About Me

Hello, my name is Bryant Flores. I am a first generation Mexican-American professional who grew up in East Los Angeles. I graduated in 2021 with a Bachelor of Science in Computer Science with a specialization in Information from the University of California Irvine. I am a father to three cats and their names are Mochi, Luna, and Kiko (short for Federico). During my free time, you can find me hiking the beautiful mountains of California, camping, and spending time looking at the Pacific Ocean on Crystal Cove state beach. On occasion, I go for runs because as a software developer I find the balance between body and mind to be crucial. I have often said to myself, that as a developer and independent thinker, if I couldn't run more than 1 mile than my mind and my thoughts would be useless.

Philosophy is one of the major things in my life that continues to push my mental limits. As a self declared existential theologist, I believe it is absolutely crucial to consider the logical conclusions of our thoughts and beliefs. I strongly believe in a God, and have always considered it a great analogy to the systems we build considering how those systems and programs themselves have creators. It seems absolutely clear to me that we must take our rhetoric and language to build a system that allows for our empathy to follow suit. As an engineer, I consider it my responsibility to have conversations with other fellow professionals about the new and upcoming rise of artificial intelligence.

I want to continue to believe in a world where there are people who want the best for our next generations. I know that it has been argued that today in this world it is objectively better and I would love to continue to believe in this optimism even if that means that we as a species are up against an ever growing uphill. I would rather not have to imagine Sisyphus happy but instead sweating and smiling, remembering that grace is always within arms reach. Especially since so many people pray and think about him. In that same way, I would love to consider us as a species dedicated to sweating and smiling all the more.

As a professional, I see myself dedicating more time to understanding a world that attempts to bring us down. I continue to advocate for people who share similar backgrounds as me and encourage them to consider a pursuit of knowledge and an application of that knowledge. The work I find myself considering for my future is largely dependent on what I can offer on a technical level, but also a personal one. In the midst of looking at the mud and darkness, I'd love to light a scented candle.

Focus Areas

My study concentrated on questions at the intersection of technology and ethics. I am observing an AI boom unfold while examining philosophical frameworks for understanding power, knowledge, and social structures. Conversations with colleagues explored connections between computational systems, ethics, and theology.

My independent research comes from the lens of a first-generation Mexican-American engineer: understanding both how technical systems function and how they impact communities underrepresented in technology development.

Guiding Questions

  • "Who builds AI systems versus who experiences their consequences?"
  • "What does technological literacy look like in a modern world and how do we reshape critical thinking skills?"
  • "How do we talk about AI ethics in communities that lack the technical background to understand what these systems are?"
  • "What responsibility do engineers have to the communities their work affects?"

Current Engagement

Recent conversations with faculty and professionals have explored the intersection of large language models and weapons technology, technological literacy gaps in underrepresented communities, and the unequal distribution of AI's benefits and harms. These discussions have confirmed the value of perspectives that bridge technical capability with lived experience outside of mainstream technology development.

My approach combines formal computer science training with self-directed philosophical study, offering a perspective on technology's impact that comes from understanding both system architecture and social consequence.
