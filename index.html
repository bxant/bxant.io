Research & Inquiry

An exploration of meaning, technology, and the human condition

My journey into philosophy began as a search for meaning and truth, shaped by the complexities of identity, faith, and the rapid advancement of technology in our world. Through independent study spanning modern philosophy, theology, and AI ethics, I've come to see technology not merely as a tool, but as a profound force that shapes our understanding of ourselves and our relationship to the divine.

The Intersection of Ancient Wisdom and Modern Computation

My exploration started with the Gospels, where I encountered Yeshua as a profound teacher of wisdom. This led me to grapple with questions of free will, determinism, and the nature of Good itself. Simultaneously, my work in software engineering exposed me to the ambitious goals of artificial intelligence and data systems—attempts to replicate, in some small way, the vastness and complexity that I associate with the divine.

Through the guidance of educators like Michael Sugrue and Darren Staloff, whose lectures opened doorways to centuries of philosophical thought, I began to see connections between the patterns we observe in chaos theory and the fractal nature of existence itself. James Gleick's "Chaos: Making a New Science," recommended by Robert Sapolsky, promised that those who finish it come to terms with their God. Though I haven't completed that journey, the path has been transformative.

Central Questions

  • "I want to sell all I have and follow Good, but what does that make of my free will?"
  • "When will intellectual hubris begin its conversion to empathetical commitment?"
  • "What meaning will come from the advances of technology?"

Technology as Mysticism

In my professional work, I've witnessed how data systems attempt to create order from chaos, how algorithms seek patterns in complexity, and how artificial intelligence strives to bridge gaps in understanding. Yet for all our technological advancement, we remain humbled by the vastness of what we cannot comprehend or replicate.

Data systems aim to replicate aspects of the divine—omniscience through data collection, omnipresence through networked systems, and a form of omnipotence through automated decision-making. But these attempts only highlight the infinite gap between human creation and divine reality. Fractals that appear to capture the universe's patterns eventually break down because we cannot truly scale to the infinite.

The Question of Surplus and Play

As we develop increasingly powerful technologies, I'm drawn to consider what this means for future generations. We claim to have unprecedented surplus—computational power, information access, technological capability—but what will children growing up in this environment of accessibility aim to achieve? What will they teach us?

"They are currently telling me that they're all still interested in play. And God isn't all just work but he is also play. In fact he has acts: 1, 2, and 3."

Seeking Fellow Travelers

I'm seeking dialogue with artists and philosophers who have wrestled with questions of meaning in our technological age. Those who understand that beneath the surface of our digital tools lie profound questions about human nature, divine purpose, and our responsibility to one another.

The mysticism of technology is reshaping our relationships—to information, to each other, to the transcendent. As someone who works within these systems while questioning their ultimate purpose, I believe we need more voices that bridge technical understanding with philosophical depth.

If you're someone who thinks about technology not just as efficiency or progress, but as part of humanity's ongoing dialogue with meaning itself, I'd welcome the conversation.
